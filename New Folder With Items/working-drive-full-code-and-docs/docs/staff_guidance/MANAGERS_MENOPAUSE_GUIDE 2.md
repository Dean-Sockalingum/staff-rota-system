# Manager's Guide: Supporting Employees Through Menopause

## Introduction

This guide provides **practical advice** for managers on supporting employees experiencing **menopause** or **perimenopause** in the workplace.

**Why this matters:**

- ‚úÖ **70% of Council workforce** are female - high likelihood you'll manage someone experiencing menopause
- ‚úÖ **Average age 51** - most women experience menopause during working life
- ‚úÖ **75% experience symptoms** - 25% have severe symptoms
- ‚úÖ **Career impact** - symptoms can affect confidence, attendance, and performance
- ‚úÖ **Retention risk** - lack of support causes women to leave jobs or reduce hours

> **üí° Key principle:** Create an environment where employees feel comfortable discussing menopause and asking for support. Small adjustments can make a big difference.

## What is Menopause?

**Definition:**

Menopause is a **biological stage** in a woman's life when she stops menstruating and reaches the end of her natural reproductive life.

**Menopause is defined as:**
- Having occurred when a woman has **not had a period for 12 consecutive months**

**Average age:** 51 years old
- **BUT:** Can be earlier or later due to surgery, illness, genetics, or other reasons

---

### The 3 Stages

| Stage | What It Means | Typical Duration |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| **1. Perimenopause** | Leading up to menopause<br>Hormonal changes begin<br>Irregular periods, symptoms start | Years before menopause<br>(can be 2-10 years) |
| **2. Menopause** | When periods have stopped for 12 consecutive months | 12 months |
| **3. Post-menopause** | After menopause<br>Some symptoms may continue<br>New health considerations | Remainder of life |

---

### Early Menopause

Some women experience menopause earlier than average:

- **Surgical menopause** - Following removal of ovaries
- **Medical treatment** - Chemotherapy, radiotherapy
- **Premature menopause** - Before age 40 (1 in 100 women)
- **Early menopause** - Before age 45 (1 in 20 women)

---

## Symptoms of Menopause

**Every woman is different:**
- Symptoms vary in type and severity
- Some women have mild symptoms, others severe
- **75% experience some symptoms**
- **25% experience severe symptoms**
- Some women have no symptoms at all

**Duration:** Symptoms can last from a few months to several years (average 4 years, but can be up to 12 years).

---

### Physical Symptoms

**Common physical symptoms include:**

| Symptom | Impact on Work |
|---------|---------------|
| **Hot flushes** | Sudden feeling of intense heat<br>Sweating, red face<br>Uncomfortable, embarrassing |
| **Night sweats** | Disrupted sleep ‚Üí fatigue at work<br>Poor concentration |
| **Heavy or irregular periods** | Need frequent toilet access<br>Risk of accidents<br>Pain, discomfort |
| **Headaches/migraines** | Difficulty concentrating<br>Need for quiet space |
| **Joint/muscle pain** | Difficulty with physical tasks<br>Mobility issues |
| **Fatigue** | Low energy, tiredness<br>Difficulty staying alert |
| **Heart palpitations** | Anxiety, distraction |

---

### Psychological/Cognitive Symptoms

| Symptom | Impact on Work |
|---------|---------------|
| **Poor concentration** | Difficulty focusing on tasks<br>Making mistakes |
| **Memory problems** | Forgetting names, appointments<br>Losing train of thought |
| **Anxiety** | Feeling nervous, worried<br>Panic attacks |
| **Low mood/depression** | Lack of motivation<br>Tearful episodes |
| **Loss of confidence** | Reluctance to speak up<br>Avoiding challenges |
| **Irritability** | Shorter temper<br>Relationship strain |
| **Difficulty sleeping** | Insomnia ‚Üí fatigue<br>Reduced performance |

---

### Impact on Work

**Menopause symptoms can affect:**

- ‚ùå **Attendance** - Increased sickness absence
- ‚ùå **Performance** - Reduced concentration, memory issues
- ‚ùå **Confidence** - Reluctance to take on new challenges
- ‚ùå **Relationships** - Irritability affecting team dynamics
- ‚ùå **Career progression** - Women may decline promotion or reduce hours
- ‚ùå **Retention** - Some women leave jobs due to lack of support

**‚ö†Ô∏è Without support, talented and experienced employees may be lost.**

## Your Role as Manager

**All line managers should:**

‚úÖ **Familiarize yourself with menopause guidance** (this document)

‚úÖ **Create a supportive working environment**
- Normalize conversations about menopause
- Be approachable and non-judgmental

‚úÖ **Be ready to have open discussions**
- Appreciate the personal nature
- Treat sensitively and professionally

‚úÖ **Discuss and agree support**
- Ask: "What would help you?"
- Agree reasonable adjustments

‚úÖ **Implement agreed adjustments**
- Act promptly
- Review regularly

---

### Responsibilities of Employees

**Employees are responsible for:**

- Taking personal responsibility for health and wellbeing
- Being open and honest about their symptoms and needs
- Raising concerns with line manager (or another manager/TU rep if more comfortable)

**If employee uncomfortable speaking to you:**
- Suggest they speak to another manager
- Trade union representative
- PAM Assist (EAP) - 0800 247 1100

## How to Have the Conversation

### Creating the Right Environment

**Before the meeting:**

‚úÖ **Allow adequate time** - Don't rush, schedule at least 30 minutes

‚úÖ **Find appropriate room** - Private, confidential, quiet

‚úÖ **Prepare yourself** - Read this guide, be ready to listen

‚úÖ **Consider who initiates** - Employee may raise it, or you may need to (sensitively) if you've noticed changes

---

### During the Conversation

**‚úÖ DO:**

1. **Listen actively**
   - Give full attention
   - Don't interrupt
   - Show empathy and understanding

2. **Encourage them to speak openly**
   - "Take your time, I'm here to listen"
   - "This is confidential"
   - "I want to help"

3. **Ask what would help**
   - "What adjustments would make things easier?"
   - "What have you found helpful in the past?"
   - "Is there anything you need from me or the team?"

4. **Discuss practical adjustments** (see list below)

5. **Agree actions**
   - What will be implemented?
   - When will it start?
   - Who needs to know?

6. **Confirm in writing** (email summary)

7. **Schedule follow-up meeting**
   - Review after 2-4 weeks
   - Adjust support as needed

---

### If You Feel Uncomfortable

**Some managers find it difficult to discuss menopause.**

**If you feel uncomfortable:**

1. **Get support from PAM Assist** (EAP)
   - Call: 0800 247 1100
   - Ask for advice on approaching difficult conversations

2. **Speak to HR** for guidance

3. **Remember:**
   - You're not expected to be a medical expert
   - Your role is to **support**, not diagnose or treat
   - The conversation is about **practical workplace support**

**Example opening:**

*"I know this might feel awkward to discuss, and I'm not a medical expert, but I want to make sure you have the support you need at work. Can we talk about what would help you?"*

## Reasonable Adjustments

**Every woman is different - adjustments should be tailored to individual needs.**

**General principle:** Small, practical changes can make a big difference.

---

### Hot Flushes

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Temperature control** | Fan on desk<br>Move workstation near window<br>Move away from heat source (radiator, kitchen) |
| **Easy access to drinking water** | Keep water bottle at desk<br>Frequent water breaks |
| **Adapt uniform** | Remove jacket<br>Lighter clothing<br>Breathable fabrics |
| **Access to rest room** | Quiet space to manage severe flush<br>Especially if role involves long periods standing/sitting |
| **Fresh air breaks** | Step outside briefly when needed |

**Example:**  
*"I'll arrange for you to have a desk fan, and I'll move your desk away from the radiator. You're welcome to remove your jacket whenever you need to, and please feel free to step outside for fresh air if you're having a hot flush."*

---

### Heavy or Irregular Periods

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Easy toilet access** | Don't restrict breaks<br>Nearby facilities<br>Frequent access if needed |
| **Extra uniform** | Spare clothing in case of accidents |
| **Flexible working** | Work from home on heavy days (if role allows) |
| **Discreet storage** | Space to keep sanitary products, spare clothes |

---

### Headaches/Migraines

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Easy access to drinking water** | Stay hydrated (reduces headaches) |
| **Quiet workspace** | Reduce noise triggers<br>Quieter area if possible |
| **Screen breaks** | Regular breaks from computer |
| **Time to take medication** | Allow time to take painkillers |
| **Dim lighting option** | Reduce bright lights if possible |

---

### Difficulty Sleeping/Fatigue

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Flexible working hours** | Later start time (if poor night's sleep)<br>Adjusted shifts |
| **Short breaks** | Power naps if possible (20 mins)<br>Fresh air breaks |
| **Reduced workload temporarily** | During particularly difficult periods |
| **Work from home** | Save commute energy (if role allows) |

---

### Poor Concentration/Memory

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Written instructions** | Don't rely on verbal only<br>Email summaries of meetings |
| **Task lists/reminders** | Help manage workload<br>Reduce anxiety about forgetting |
| **Quiet workspace** | Reduce distractions<br>Better focus |
| **Break tasks into smaller chunks** | Less overwhelming<br>Easier to complete |
| **Extra time for complex tasks** | Reduce pressure |

---

### Anxiety/Low Mood

**Adjustments to consider:**

| Adjustment | How It Helps |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Regular check-ins** | 1:1 meetings for support<br>Opportunity to raise concerns |
| **Access to natural light/fresh air** | Short breaks outside<br>Desk near window |
| **Reduced pressured deadlines** | During difficult periods |
| **Flexible working** | Control over work environment |
| **Reassurance and support** | Positive feedback<br>Encouragement |
| **Refer to EAP** | Professional counseling support |

---

### When Adjustments Aren't Enough

**If agreed adjustments are unsuccessful, or symptoms are more problematic:**

1. **Refer to Occupational Health**
   - Medical assessment
   - Expert advice on further adjustments
   - See: [Manager's Guide: OH Referrals](./MANAGERS_OH_REFERRAL_GUIDE.md)

2. **Consider extended support**
   - Phased adjustments over longer period
   - Combination of multiple adjustments
   - More significant changes (redeployment, reduced hours)

3. **Monitor sickness absence**
   - If menopause symptoms causing frequent absence
   - **Be sympathetic** - don't discipline for genuine health-related absence
   - Explore adjustments to **reduce** absence

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Employee Confides She's Struggling

**Situation:**
Care assistant (age 48) asks to speak to you privately. She's tearful and says she's been having hot flushes, poor sleep, and feels like she can't cope. She's embarrassed and worried about her job.

**Your response:**

1. ‚úÖ **Thank her for sharing**
   - "Thank you for telling me. I know this isn't easy to talk about."

2. ‚úÖ **Reassure her**
   - "You're not alone - many women experience this. We'll work together to support you."
   - "Your job is not at risk - we just need to find what will help."

3. ‚úÖ **Ask what would help**
   - "What do you think would make things easier for you at work?"

4. ‚úÖ **Discuss practical adjustments**
   - Desk fan for hot flushes
   - Flexible start time if poor night's sleep
   - Regular check-ins for support

5. ‚úÖ **Suggest support services**
   - GP (may prescribe HRT or other treatment)
   - PAM Assist for counseling (0800 247 1100)

6. ‚úÖ **Agree actions and follow-up**
   - Implement adjustments this week
   - Review in 2 weeks

**Example outcome:**  
*"I'll arrange a desk fan for you today, and we'll move you to a desk near the window so you can get fresh air easily. If you've had a bad night's sleep, text me in the morning and we can adjust your start time. I'd also suggest speaking to your GP about treatment options, and PAM Assist can provide counseling support if you need someone to talk to. Let's meet again in 2 weeks to see how you're getting on."*

---

### Scenario 2: Male Employee Asks About Supporting Partner

**Situation:**
Male team leader asks to speak to you about his wife who is experiencing severe menopause symptoms. He wants to understand so he can support her, and asks if flexible working might help him (to support her at home).

**Your response:**

1. ‚úÖ **Appreciate him asking**
   - "I'm glad you asked - it shows you care and want to support your wife."

2. ‚úÖ **Provide information**
   - Give him this guide or links to NHS/menopause resources
   - Explain symptoms and support options

3. ‚úÖ **Discuss his flexible working request**
   - Consider if/how flexible working could be accommodated
   - See: [Flexible Working Procedure](link)

4. ‚úÖ **Suggest support for his wife (if she works for Council)**
   - She can speak to her manager about adjustments
   - PAM Assist available to her too

**Example outcome:**  
*"It's great that you want to support your wife. Here's information about menopause that might help you understand what she's experiencing. Regarding flexible working, let's discuss what would work for both you and the team. I think we can arrange for you to start later twice a week if that would help. If your wife works for the Council, encourage her to speak to her manager about support at work too."*

---

### Scenario 3: You've Noticed Changes in Performance

**Situation:**
You've noticed employee (admin officer, age 52) is making more mistakes than usual, seems forgetful, and has become tearful on two occasions. You're concerned about her wellbeing and work quality.

**‚ö†Ô∏è Approach sensitively - don't assume it's menopause.**

**Your response:**

1. ‚úÖ **Arrange confidential meeting**
   - "I'd like to have a chat with you - I've noticed you seem a bit stressed lately and I want to check if you're okay."

2. ‚úÖ **Open the conversation gently**
   - "I've noticed [specific examples: errors in reports, tearful] and I'm a bit concerned. Is everything okay? Is there anything going on that I should know about?"

3. ‚úÖ **Listen without judgment**
   - She may disclose menopause, or something else entirely
   - Don't assume

4. ‚úÖ **If she discloses menopause:**
   - "Thank you for sharing that with me. I want to support you."
   - Discuss adjustments (see above)

5. ‚úÖ **If she doesn't disclose:**
   - Offer support generally
   - "If there's anything affecting your work, I'm here to help. We have support available through PAM Assist if you'd like to talk to someone confidentially."

6. ‚úÖ **Address performance sensitively**
   - Don't discipline if health-related
   - Focus on support first

**Example outcome:**  
*"Thank you for being honest with me about the menopause symptoms you're experiencing. I understand that poor concentration and memory can be really frustrating. Let's make some changes to help: I'll make sure you get written instructions for tasks and email summaries after meetings, and I'll move you to a quieter desk to help with concentration. I'd also suggest speaking to your GP about treatment options. Let's review in a month to see if these adjustments are helping."*

---

### Scenario 4: Employee Requests Frequent Absence for Symptoms

**Situation:**
Employee has requested several days off sick for menopause symptoms (hot flushes, fatigue) over the past 3 months. She's worried about hitting absence triggers.

**Your response:**

1. ‚úÖ **Be sympathetic**
   - "I know menopause symptoms can be really debilitating."

2. ‚úÖ **Explore adjustments to **reduce** absence**
   - "Let's talk about what might help you manage symptoms at work so you don't need to take time off."
   - Discuss adjustments (flexible working, temperature control, breaks, etc.)

3. ‚úÖ **Reassure about absence policy**
   - If symptoms are causing frequent absence, be understanding
   - **Don't discipline for genuine health-related absence**
   - Consider OH referral for advice

4. ‚úÖ **Refer to OH if needed**
   - Medical assessment
   - Advice on adjustments
   - May recommend reasonable adjustments or temporary measures

5. ‚úÖ **Encourage medical support**
   - GP may prescribe HRT or other treatments
   - May significantly improve symptoms

**Example outcome:**  
*"I understand your symptoms are severe, and I don't want you to worry about absence triggers when you're genuinely unwell. However, let's see if we can make adjustments that help you manage symptoms at work so you don't need to take time off. I'm going to refer you to Occupational Health for expert advice on adjustments, and I'd also encourage you to speak to your GP about treatment options like HRT which can really help. In the meantime, let's try flexible working hours and temperature control to see if that makes a difference."*

---

### Scenario 5: Team Member Makes Insensitive Comments

**Situation:**
You overhear a male colleague make a joke about a female team member's menopause symptoms ("Must be the time of the month... or year!"). The woman looks upset and embarrassed.

**Your response:**

1. ‚úÖ **Address immediately**
   - "That's not appropriate. Menopause is a health issue and we don't make jokes about people's health."

2. ‚úÖ **Speak to the colleague privately**
   - Explain why comment was unacceptable
   - Educate about menopause
   - Warn that further comments may be disciplinary matter (bullying/harassment)

3. ‚úÖ **Check in with affected employee**
   - "I'm sorry you had to hear that. It wasn't acceptable. Are you okay?"
   - Offer support

4. ‚úÖ **Consider team education**
   - Brief team meeting about menopause awareness
   - Create supportive culture
   - Emphasize zero tolerance for jokes/stigma

**Example outcome:**  
*"I've spoken to [colleague] about his comment - it was inappropriate and won't happen again. I want to make sure our workplace is supportive and respectful for everyone. If you experience any further comments like that, please let me know immediately. In the meantime, how are you doing? Is there any support you need?"*

## Best Practice Tips

### ‚úÖ DO:

| DO | Why It Matters |
|----|----------------|
| **Educate yourself about menopause** | Understand symptoms and impact<br>Better support your team |
| **Create open, supportive culture** | Normalizes menopause<br>Employees feel safe to ask for help |
| **Listen without judgment** | Shows respect<br>Builds trust |
| **Take symptoms seriously** | They're real and can be severe<br>Don't minimize experience |
| **Be flexible** | Small adjustments make big difference<br>Costs little, retains talent |
| **Ask "What would help you?"** | Employee knows their needs best<br>Personalized support |
| **Act promptly on agreed adjustments** | Shows you take it seriously<br>Provides relief quickly |
| **Maintain confidentiality** | Personal health information<br>Only share with consent |
| **Review regularly** | Symptoms change over time<br>Adjust support as needed |
| **Signpost to support services** | GP, PAM Assist, OH<br>Professional support available |
| **Challenge stigma and jokes** | Creates respectful culture<br>Protects employees |

---

### ‚ùå DON'T:

| DON'T | Why It's a Problem |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Don't assume all women experience same symptoms** | Every woman different<br>Personalize support |
| **Don't make jokes or dismiss symptoms** | Disrespectful, hurtful<br>Creates hostile environment |
| **Don't share information without consent** | Breaches confidentiality<br>Breaks trust |
| **Don't ignore signs of struggling** | Early support prevents escalation<br>May prevent absence |
| **Don't discipline for symptom-related issues** | Performance drops may be temporary<br>Support first, not punish |
| **Don't make assumptions about age** | Some women experience early menopause<br>Don't rule out based on age |
| **Don't refuse reasonable adjustments** | Legal risk if disability<br>Loses skilled employees |
| **Don't treat as "women's problem"** | Affects whole team<br>Men can support too |
| **Don't be embarrassed to discuss** | If you're awkward, they'll feel awkward<br>Normalize the conversation |
